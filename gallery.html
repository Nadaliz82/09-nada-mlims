In general, ancient tifinagh is considered to be very close to the current tifinagh of Ahaggar (Algeria) and Ajjer (Algeria/Libya) (Claudot Award: 2011 ), and today's Tuaregs are able to read and interpret it.

Table of Tifinaghs (Tuareg alphabet), from Agali Zakara (1984)

Indeed, it was the Tuaregs who continued to use Tifinagh and it is thanks to them that this alphabet was able to survive until today.One can distinguish slight differences from one region to another , but even so, texts written in one region can be easily read and understood in another.On the other hand, Tifinagh characters have been used to decorate both women's jewelry and men's weapons and also to write small texts and love poems.

The “Oriental” Libyan alphabet according to J.-B. Chabot

Tifinagh is a consonantal alphabet that has not ceased to evolve throughout history while maintaining consistency at the morphological and structural level.In the Libyan writing composed of 24 signs , the vowels are not transcribed unlike the semi-vowels (y, w) and we ignore the doubling of consonants and the indication of tension (Camps: 2011) .

Inscription from the mausoleum of Atban (Libyan text).British Museum.Photo Sophie A. de Beaune

Tifinagh/tifinaɣ is a very old writing system used by the Numidians in North Africa since antiquity.Also called Libyan, this alphabet was able to survive and reach us through the native inhabitants of this vast region who continued to use it.Through a large number of inscriptions still preserved , useful information about the language has been revealed claiming that these ancient forms evolved into the current Tifinagh (Camps, 1992).

The man with the inscription, engraving from Azib n'Ikkis (Photo J. Malhomme)

Concerning the origins of the tifinagh, the problem is still not settled.Casajus (2013) estimates that the Libyans would have created their writing system between the 6th and 4th centuries BC using several letters used at the time by the Phoenicians and the Punics and creating more.Amara (2020: 541) talks about a centuries-long gap in the estimation of ancient Libyan scriptures.She cites the parietal inscription of Azib n'Ikkis du Yagour (Fig. 2) discovered in the Moroccan High Atlas by Malhomme in 1959, the dating of which has been placed at the Bronze II age.
